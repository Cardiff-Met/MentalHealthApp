# 1. Introduction

This document presents five user personas and five corresponding usage scenarios for the Personalized Mental Health Support App.

These personas and scenarios refine product scope, validate the prototype created in Week 2, and prepare for user story development in Week 4.

# 2. Personas

---

## Persona 1 – Primary User

**Name:** Emily Jones
**Age:** 20
**Background:** Second-year university student studying Psychology in Cardiff. Lives in student accommodation and balances coursework with a part-time job.

### Goals and Motivations
- Manage stress during busy academic periods
- Access mental health resources privately
- Quickly request support sessions when needed

### Frustrations / Pain Points
- Long waiting times for counselling
- Difficulty finding reliable, student-specific support
- Anxiety about privacy when seeking help

### Technical Skill Level
**Medium**
Confident using mobile apps and web platforms but not technical.

### Context of Use
- Uses mobile phone most often
- Logs in during evenings or late at night
- Often under time pressure during assessment periods

### Relationship to the Product
Emily would use the app regularly for mood tracking and resource discovery.
Success looks like:
"I can quickly log how I feel and get helpful suggestions without feeling judged."

---

## Persona 2 – Secondary User

**Name:** Daniel Evans
**Age:** 24
**Background:** Postgraduate Engineering student. Recently relocated to Cardiff and unfamiliar with local support services.

### Goals and Motivations
- Find local mental health services
- Request appointments efficiently
- Understand what services are available and how booking works

### Frustrations / Pain Points
- Confusion about where to go for support
- Unclear service eligibility
- Limited time due to research commitments

### Technical Skill Level
**High**
Comfortable navigating digital systems and online booking platforms.

### Context of Use
- Uses laptop during daytime
- Needs structured, reliable information
- Often multitasking between research and coursework

### Relationship to the Product
Daniel would primarily use the service information and booking request features.
Success looks like:
"I can identify the right service and request an appointment in minutes."

## Persona 3 – Edge-Case User

**Name:** Aisha Khan
**Age:** 19

### Goals and Motivations
- Understand how to access help safely
- Feel supported without complex processes
- Use simple, clear interfaces

### Frustrations / Pain Points
- Complex language in service descriptions
- Fear of stigma
- Low confidence in navigating unfamiliar systems

### Technical Skill Level

Uses social media and messaging apps but struggles with complex navigation.

### Context of Use
- Uses smartphone only
- May access the app in shared accommodation
- Concerned about privacy

### Relationship to the Product
Aisha needs reassurance and clarity.

## Persona 4 – Mature / Part-Time Student

**Name:** Marcus Williams
**Age:** 34
**Background:** Part-time mature student studying Business Administration while working full-time and caring for a young child. Commutes to Cardiff two days a week.

### Goals and Motivations
- Find mental health support that fits around a demanding schedule
- Access resources quickly without lengthy sign-up or navigation
- Book appointments at times that accommodate work and family commitments

### Frustrations / Pain Points
- Standard university support hours clash with work and childcare
- Feels disconnected from student services designed for younger, full-time students
- Limited time means he abandons complex or slow processes quickly
- Low confidence using new apps — prefers clear, familiar layouts

### Technical Skill Level
**Low to Medium**
Comfortable with email and basic web browsing but not confident with new digital platforms.

### Context of Use
- Uses a laptop at home in the evenings or early mornings
- Has very limited time windows — typically 10–15 minutes at a time
- Rarely on campus so in-person support is difficult to access

### Relationship to the Product
Marcus needs fast, straightforward access to resources and booking without a steep learning curve.
Success looks like:
"I can find what I need and book an appointment in the time it takes to make a cup of tea."

---

## Persona 5 – Student in Crisis / Urgent Need

**Name:** Sophie Davies
**Age:** 21
**Background:** Third-year student studying Law. Has been managing anxiety for two years but is currently experiencing a period of acute distress following a family bereavement and exam pressure.

### Goals and Motivations
- Find immediate support or guidance during a mental health crisis
- Understand what to do and who to contact urgently
- Feel that the app takes her situation seriously

### Frustrations / Pain Points
- Standard booking workflows feel too slow when in acute distress
- Uncertainty about whether her situation is "serious enough" to seek urgent help
- Fear of being dismissed or put on a long waiting list
- Not knowing the difference between crisis support and general counselling

### Technical Skill Level
**Medium**
Can use apps effectively when calm, but during distress she needs minimal steps and clear signposting.

### Context of Use
- Could access the app at any time of day, including late at night
- May be on a mobile phone in a private space
- Cognitive load is high — needs the app to do as much thinking for her as possible

### Relationship to the Product
Sophie needs the app to recognise urgency, surface crisis resources immediately, and direct her to the right help without requiring her to navigate complex menus.
Success looks like:
"I found the help I needed quickly without having to figure out what to click."

# 3. Usage Scenarios

---

## Scenario 1 – Emily (Primary User) — Mood logging under pressure

**Starting Situation:**
Emily has two deadlines next week and feels overwhelmed.

**Trigger:**
She experiences anxiety symptoms late at night.

**Goal:**
Log her mood and receive personalised support suggestions.

**What She Does (High-Level Steps):**
1. Logs into the app
2. Selects "Record Mood"
3. Rates her mood and adds a short description
4. Submits entry
5. Views recommended resources

**Challenge/Constraint:**
She is tired and wants a fast, simple process.

**Outcome (Success):**
She receives tailored coping resources and feels reassured.

---

## Scenario 2 – Daniel (Secondary User) — Requesting a session with external confirmation

**Starting Situation:**
Daniel realises he needs professional support after prolonged stress.

**Trigger:**
A friend suggests contacting university counselling.

**Goal:**
Request an appointment slot and receive confirmation.

**What He Does (High-Level Steps):**
1. Logs into the app
2. Navigates to the booking calendar
3. Reviews available slots and chooses a suitable time
4. Submits the booking request
5. Checks booking status (Pending → Confirmed/Declined)

**Challenge/Constraint:**
He has limited time and needs quick clarity; the first chosen slot may not be confirmed.

**Outcome (Success):**
His request is confirmed and he receives appointment details; if declined, he quickly selects another slot.

---

## Scenario 3 – Aisha (Edge-Case User) — Exploring support privately

**Starting Situation:**
Aisha feels isolated during her first semester.

**Trigger:**
She sees a university wellbeing poster recommending the app.

**Goal:**
Explore available support safely and privately.

**What She Does (High-Level Steps):**
1. Registers for an account
2. Logs in
3. Browses the resources page
4. Reads beginner-friendly guidance
5. Saves a helpful resource

**Challenge/Constraint:**
Concerned about privacy and unclear terminology.

**Outcome (Success):**
She understands her options and feels confident seeking help.

---

## Scenario 4 – Marcus (Mature/Part-Time Student) — Quick access in a limited time window

**Starting Situation:**
Marcus has had a stressful week balancing work deadlines with an essay submission. He has 15 minutes before his child wakes up.

**Trigger:**
He notices he has been feeling low for several days and decides to finally do something about it.

**Goal:**
Log his mood and book a therapy session at an evening or weekend slot that fits around work.

**What He Does (High-Level Steps):**
1. Opens the app on his laptop
2. Logs in quickly using saved credentials
3. Records his mood with a low rating and brief note
4. Navigates to the booking calendar
5. Filters available slots by evening availability
6. Submits a booking request for a suitable evening slot

**Challenge/Constraint:**
He has very little time and will abandon the process if it requires too many steps or is confusing. He is also less familiar with the app than a younger student would be.

**Outcome (Success):**
He successfully logs his mood and submits a booking request within his 15-minute window, with a clear confirmation that his request is pending.

---

## Scenario 5 – Sophie (Student in Crisis) — Finding urgent support during acute distress

**Starting Situation:**
Sophie is at home late at night feeling overwhelmed and unable to cope. She opens the app looking for help.

**Trigger:**
She rates her mood as 1 (the lowest) and adds a description indicating she is in acute distress.

**Goal:**
Be directed immediately to crisis resources and emergency contact information without needing to navigate multiple screens.

**What She Does (High-Level Steps):**
1. Logs into the app
2. Records a mood rating of 1 with a distress description
3. Is immediately shown a crisis support banner/alert on screen
4. Reads the signposting to Samaritans, university out-of-hours crisis line, and NHS urgent mental health support
5. Decides to call the Samaritans number displayed

**Challenge/Constraint:**
Sophie is in distress and cannot manage complex navigation. She needs the app to respond to her low mood rating immediately and surface urgent help without her having to search for it.

**Outcome (Success):**
The app detects the low mood entry and automatically surfaces a clearly visible crisis support panel with contact numbers, without requiring additional navigation. Sophie feels the app took her situation seriously.

# 4. Traceability: Scenario → Prototype Screens

---

## Scenario 1 (Emily) — Mood Logging

Supported by:
- Login Screen
- Mood Input Screen
- Dashboard
- Resources Page

How Screens Help:
- Mood input allows emotional logging with minimal friction
- Dashboard shows personalised suggestions immediately
- Resources provide actionable coping guidance

---

## Scenario 2 (Daniel) — Booking Request + Status

Supported by:
- Login Screen
- Booking Calendar Page (request booking)
- Booking Status / Confirmation Page
- Dashboard (booking summary)

How Screens Help:
- Calendar supports choosing an available slot quickly
- Status page reduces uncertainty (Pending/Confirmed/Declined)
- Dashboard provides at-a-glance view of upcoming confirmed sessions

---

## Scenario 3 (Aisha) — Private Resource Discovery

Supported by:
- Registration Screen
- Login Screen
- Resources Page

How Screens Help:
- Simple registration supports accessibility
- Resources screen can be designed with clear language and privacy reassurance
- Secure login reduces anxiety about confidentiality

---

## Scenario 4 (Marcus) — Quick Access in Limited Time

Supported by:
- Login Screen (with remembered credentials)
- Mood Input Screen
- Booking Calendar Page (with time-of-day filtering)
- Booking Status / Confirmation Page

How Screens Help:
- Fast login reduces friction for a time-pressured user
- Mood input is minimal and quick to complete
- Calendar filtering helps Marcus find realistic evening slots without scrolling through unsuitable times
- Confirmation page gives immediate reassurance that the request was received

---

## Scenario 5 (Sophie) — Crisis Support Signposting

Supported by:
- Login Screen
- Mood Input Screen (triggers crisis detection on rating of 1)
- Crisis Support Panel (new screen / overlay — identified as a backlog gap)
- Dashboard

How Screens Help:
- Mood input is the trigger point for crisis detection
- A dedicated crisis support panel or overlay surfaces immediately after a critically low mood entry
- Dashboard should show a persistent "Need urgent help?" banner for users who have logged a crisis-level mood

**Backlog Gap Identified:** A dedicated Crisis Support screen or overlay is not currently represented in the Week 2 prototype and should be added as a new screen requirement.

---

# 5. Insights and Backlog Notes

- Booking must provide clear status visibility (Pending vs Confirmed) to reduce anxiety
- Declined requests should have a fast "choose another slot" recovery path
- Simple language improves accessibility for international students and edge users
- Privacy reassurance should be emphasised during registration and booking
- Time filtering on the booking calendar is needed to support part-time and working students (Marcus)
- A crisis detection trigger on mood rating of 1 is needed, surfacing emergency contacts immediately (Sophie)
- A Crisis Support screen / overlay is a missing prototype screen identified through Scenario 5 — this should be added to the backlog as a Must Have feature given the nature of the product

These insights will inform user story creation in Week 4.
